Dear Dr. Jack,

I am writing to inquire about a potential submission to *Preventing Chronic Disease* for the January 2026 call for papers, *Geospatial Perspectives on the Intersection of Chronic Disease, Risk Factors, and Health Outcomes*.

I am a PhD candidate in Health Systems and Services Research at the University of Arkansas for Medical Sciences, Department of Health Policy and Management, completing dissertation work on the geography of diabetic foot ulcer (DFU) burden in Arkansas using the Arkansas All-Payer Claims Database (2017–2024). I was also a PCD Student Scientific Writing and Review Committee appointee from August 2024 through April 2025, which gave me a close look at the journal's scope and standards — and a strong interest in placing this work with PCD.

**Brief description of the proposed paper:**

- **Aim:** Characterize ZCTA-level geographic variation and space-time clustering of DFU among Arkansans with diabetes, 2017–2024.
- **Data:** AR APCD, covering ~365,000 diabetics across commercial and Medicare payers; linked at the person level via APCD unique ID.
- **Methods:**
  - Tiered claims-based DFU case definitions (L97.x alone; L97.x with debridement CPT within ±30 days [primary]; DM-ulcer combination codes [E10.62x/E11.62x]).
  - Static spatial clustering: Global Moran's I, Local Getis-Ord Gi*, LISA.
  - **Emerging Hot Spot Analysis (EHSA)** in ArcGIS Pro using an annual ZCTA space-time cube to classify ZCTAs as new, consecutive, intensifying, persistent, or diminishing hot/cold spots.
- **Preliminary findings:** Significant global spatial autocorrelation (Moran's I = 0.044, p = 0.009); 41 static hot spots concentrated in the Arkansas Delta, consistent with known rural health disparities in the region. EHSA analysis is in progress.
- **Public health relevance:** Results are intended to inform targeting of wound care resources, FQHC podiatry services, and rural chronic disease surveillance priorities in Arkansas.

The paper directly responds to the call's interest in "maps illustrating geographic variations in chronic disease and health outcomes" and the use of GIS by state health systems in rural settings.

**My question:** Given the map-centric nature of the analysis and the call's explicit interest in geospatial visualization, would this work be better suited as an **Original Research** article or a **GIS Snapshot**? I want to ensure I format the submission to fit the journal's expectations before the May 1 deadline.

I would be grateful for any guidance, and I am happy to provide additional detail on the analytic approach or preliminary results if useful.

Thank you for your time, and for the opportunity the Student Scientific Writing and Review Committee provided — it remains one of the most formative experiences of my doctoral training.
